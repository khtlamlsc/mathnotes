The cube and the cylinder

An animated comparison of partial vs full submersion.

Problem setup

A right circular cylindrical vessel has base radius 16 cm. A metal cube of side 12 cm sinks to the bottom of the vessel, with no overflow. Two cases are compared:

  • Case A — initial water depth 9 cm. Result: cube partially submerged (top exposed).
  • Case B — initial water depth 12 cm. Result: cube fully submerged.

Case A — partial submersion (initial water 9 cm)

Working

Volume of water: Vw = π × 16² × 9 = 2304π cm³ ≈ 7238 cm³.

First test the "fully submerged" assumption — what depth would that imply?

h  =  (2304π + 1728) ÷ 256π  ≈  11.15 cm

But 11.15 cm < 12 cm (cube height), so the assumption is impossible. The cube must be partially submerged. Water occupies the annular region around the cube:

Vw = (256π − 144) × h  ⇒  h = 2304π / (256π − 144) ≈ 10.96 cm

Answer: water depth ≈ 11.0 cm (3 s.f.).

Case B — full submersion (initial water 12 cm)

Working

Volume of water: Vw = π × 16² × 12 = 3072π cm³ ≈ 9651 cm³.

Test the "fully submerged" assumption:

h  =  (3072π + 1728) ÷ 256π  =  12 + 1728 / (256π) ≈ 14.15 cm

Since 14.15 cm ≥ 12 cm, the assumption holds — the cube is genuinely fully submerged. Water rises by 14.15 − 12 = 2.15 cm.

Answer: water depth ≈ 14.1 cm (3 s.f.).

Notice in the animation: as the cube descends, the water level stops rising the moment the cube becomes fully submerged. After that point, additional descent doesn't displace any new water.

Key formulas to remember

Partial submersion:   h = Vwater ÷ (Avessel − Acube cross-section)
Full submersion:   h = (Vwater + Vcube) ÷ Avessel
  • Always test the "full submersion" assumption first. Compute h using the full-submersion formula. If h ≥ cube height, you're done. If not, switch to the partial-submersion formula.
  • Critical threshold for this problem: initial water depth ≥ 12 − 1728/(256π) ≈ 9.85 cm guarantees full submersion. Case A at 9 cm sits just below this cutoff; Case B at 12 cm sits comfortably above.
  • Sanity check: the new water depth must be greater than the original (water can only rise when an object is added) and at most equal to the cube's height plus a constant rise — never less than what conservation predicts.
